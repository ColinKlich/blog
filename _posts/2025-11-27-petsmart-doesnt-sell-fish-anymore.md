I left my aquarium alone for the summer. It was a shrimp-only tank, and while it may sound neglectful to let it sit for three months, I knew it would be fine.

Shrimp are hardy. They're self-cleaning. They don't need constant attention.

What I didn't account for was the conversation with a friend that would change everything I thought I knew about the pet industry.

## The Shocking Discovery

When I got back, I stopped by PetSmart to pick up some supplies. I've been a regular for years, always buying shrimp food, water conditioner, and test kits.

But when I walked into the fish section, I noticed something strange.

**There were no fish.**

Not one. Not even in the tiny tanks I remembered seeing before. Just rows and rows of empty aquariums, each one pristine and untouched.

I asked the employee at the counter. "What happened to all the fish?"

She looked at me like I was asking why the moon doesn't orbit the Earth. "We don't sell fish anymore."

**Three months ago, they stopped selling fish entirely.**

## The Why

Turns out, it wasn't a temporary shortage or a seasonal thing. This was a **permanent strategic decision**.

According to the company, they're pivoting to focus on "high-value companion animals" — dogs, cats, and other mammals that generate more revenue and have stronger customer relationships.

Fish? They're apparently:
- Low margin
- High return rate (people buy, they die, they return for refunds)
- Require more education and support
- Don't create the same ongoing revenue stream

But here's the thing: **we need fish**.

Not as pets, but as living organisms that teach us about ecosystems, patience, and stewardship. Fish tanks are:
- More affordable than dogs or cats
- Better for small spaces
- Quieter (no barking, no scratching)
- Lower ongoing cost (food, vet bills, boarding)
- Perfect for people who can't commit to high-maintenance animals

## The Bigger Picture

This isn't just about fish. It's about **what we value as a society**.

When a company with thousands of stores decides fish aren't worth selling, they're sending a message:
- Complex life forms aren't valuable unless they're expensive
- Animals that don't generate maximum profit aren't worth keeping
- The ecosystem that connects people to nature is less important than quarterly earnings

But fish in a home aquarium are more than just decoration. They're:
- Living ecosystems you maintain
- Lessons in patience and observation
- Stress relief through watching them swim
- Educational opportunities for children
- Affordable entry into pet ownership

## The Alternative Landscape

The sad truth is that while PetSmart stopped selling fish, **demand for fish hasn't disappeared**. People still want them. They still need them. They still benefit from owning them.

So where do they get them?
- Small local fish stores (disappearing due to competition)
- Online retailers (shipping live animals is risky)
- Breeder networks (harder to find and verify)
- Other big box stores (limited options)

The result? **Less access to quality fish, higher prices, and fewer educational opportunities for people who could benefit from fish ownership.**

## What This Means for Us

This decision by PetSmart reveals something about our priorities:
- **Profit over accessibility** — They could sell fish profitably, they just choose not to because they'd make more selling dogs
- **Profit over education** — They're not investing in teaching people how to care for fish
- **Profit over ecosystem health** — They're removing a piece of the aquatic ecosystem they could support

But it also shows us something else: **the power of choice**.

PetSmart decided to stop selling fish. That's their business decision. But we get to decide whether to accept that limitation or find other ways to get what we need.

## The Lesson

Here's what I took away from this:

**1. Not everything valuable is profitable**
   Fish are valuable, but not as profitable as dogs. The market doesn't always reward what's good.

**2. Corporate decisions affect access**
   When one big player makes a decision, it ripples through the entire ecosystem, making things harder for everyone.

**3. You can work around corporate decisions**
   PetSmart stopped selling fish, but people still keep aquariums. They find other ways. That's the point of resourcefulness.

**4. Value isn't just about profit**
   Just because something isn't profitable doesn't mean it's not valuable. Sometimes it's the opposite.

## The Fish Still Need Us

The shrimp in my tank? They're still there. Thriving. Unaware that their species has lost a major retail partner.

But I'm aware. And I think about the kids who won't get to learn about responsibility and ecosystems through a fish tank because the big store decided to stop selling fish.

I think about the elderly people who find peace watching fish but can't afford a dog. I think about people in small apartments who want a pet but can't handle a high-maintenance animal. I think about all the educational opportunities that just disappeared.

## What You Can Do

If you want fish but can't find them at PetSmart:
- **Support local fish stores** — They're often struggling and need your help
- **Find online retailers** — Look for highly-rated breeders
- **Join aquarium communities** — They often know where to find fish
- **Ask your local pet store** — Smaller stores may still carry fish
- **Consider starting your own ecosystem** — Even a small bowl is better than nothing

## The Bottom Line

PetSmart decided fish aren't worth selling. That's their call.

But fish are still worth keeping. And the people who keep them are still worth supporting.

Don't let a corporate decision define what's valuable in your life.

If you want a fish tank, find a way to get one. The fish will thank you.
